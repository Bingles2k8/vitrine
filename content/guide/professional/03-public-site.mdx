## A full museum website

On Professional and above, your Vitrine site is a complete, multi-page public website: not just a collection browser, but a proper online home for your institution. Visitors land on a homepage with your featured objects and branding, then navigate to a collection browser, an About page, a Visit page, and (if you use ticketing) an Events page.

Every page is mobile-optimised, accessible, and loads quickly. You don't need a web developer to set any of it up.

<ProPublicSiteMockup />

Your site includes:

- **Home**: your museum name, tagline, description, and featured objects
- **Collection**: a searchable, filterable browser of all your published objects
- **About**: your museum's story, team, mission, and facilities
- **Visit**: opening hours, admission details, address, accessibility info, and directions
- **Events**: upcoming events with online booking (requires ticketing to be set up)

---

## The Site Builder

Everything about your public website is controlled from **Site Builder** in the dashboard. It's divided into tabs:

- **Appearance**: template, accent colour, and logo
- **Content**: tagline, description, about text, featured objects, social links, and SEO
- **Visit info**: opening hours, admission prices, accessibility, and directions

Changes are previewed in a live panel on the right. Click **Publish** in the top-right corner to push your changes to the live site.

## Writing your About page

The About page gives visitors context about your museum: its history, its collection focus, the people behind it, and its facilities. It's also one of the first things funders and grant bodies look at.

<Steps title="How do I write my About page?">
  <Step number={1}>Go to **Site Builder → Content**, then scroll down to the **About** section, or click the **About** tab if one is shown.</Step>
  <Step number={2}>Write your museum's story in the main text area. You can use basic formatting: headings, bold text, italic, and bullet lists. Keep it readable: a few well-chosen paragraphs are more effective than a wall of text.</Step>
  <Step number={3}>Optionally fill in the **Facilities** field, listing practical things visitors might want to know: café, shop, learning spaces, accessible toilets, hearing loop, etc.</Step>
  <Step number={4}>Click **Publish** to save and apply the changes to your live site.</Step>
</Steps>

## Setting up your Visit page

The Visit page is often the most practically useful page on a museum website. Visitors check it before travelling to make sure the museum is open and to plan their journey. Keeping it accurate and up to date is one of the highest-value things you can do.

<Steps title="How do I set up my Visit page?">
  <Step number={1}>Go to **Site Builder → Visit info**.</Step>
  <Step number={2}>Fill in your **Opening hours**. Describe your schedule in plain text (e.g. "Tuesday to Sunday, 10:00–17:00. Closed Mondays and Bank Holidays").</Step>
  <Step number={3}>Enter your **Admission** details (e.g. "Free entry" or "Adults £8, Children £4, Under-5s free").</Step>
  <Step number={4}>Add your **Address**: the full postal address, formatted as it would appear on an envelope.</Step>
  <Step number={5}>Write an **Accessibility** note covering any relevant information: step-free access, induction loops, large print guides, accessible parking, etc. Be as specific as possible; vague statements like "partially accessible" aren't helpful to visitors with specific needs.</Step>
  <Step number={6}>Add transport and parking information in the **Getting here** field: nearby bus stops, train stations, and car parks.</Step>
  <Step number={7}>Click **Publish** to save your changes.</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  Set a reminder in your calendar to review the Visit page whenever your hours change, especially around Christmas, bank holidays, or temporary closures. Out-of-date visit information is one of the most common and frustrating problems visitors encounter on museum websites.
</Callout>

<Steps title="How do I add a temporary closure or special notice?">
  <Step number={1}>Go to **Site Builder → Visit info**.</Step>
  <Step number={2}>Find the **Special notices** field. Type your notice here, e.g. "Closed 24–26 December and 1 January" or "The Roman Gallery is temporarily closed for redisplay until March."</Step>
  <Step number={3}>Click **Publish**. The notice appears prominently at the top of your Visit page.</Step>
  <Step number={4}>When the closure ends, return to this field, clear the notice, and publish again.</Step>
</Steps>

## SEO: helping people find you on Google

Vitrine sites are structured to be picked up by search engines by default, but you can improve your search performance by setting a custom meta title and description.

<Steps title="How do I set my site's SEO title and description?">
  <Step number={1}>Go to **Site Builder → Content**, then scroll to the **SEO** section.</Step>
  <Step number={2}>Set the **Meta title**: this appears in the browser tab and as the clickable headline in Google search results. A good format is "Museum Name | Short description", e.g. "Victoria Hamlet Textiles Collection | Textiles & History".</Step>
  <Step number={3}>Set the **Meta description**: this is the text snippet shown under your link in search results. Write 1–2 sentences that accurately describe your museum and make someone want to click. Keep it under 160 characters.</Step>
  <Step number={4}>Click **Publish**. Changes are reflected immediately on your site, though Google and other search engines may take a few days to re-index.</Step>
</Steps>
