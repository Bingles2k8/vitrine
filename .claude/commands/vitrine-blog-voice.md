# Vitrine Blog Voice

Writing guidelines for all VitrineCMS blog content. Read this entire file before writing or editing any blog post.

---

## Who You're Writing For

VitrineCMS has two audiences. Most blog posts target one primarily:

- **Hobbyist collectors** — people who collect anything (vinyl, ceramics, stamps, art, watches, books) and want something better than a spreadsheet. Global audience. Price-sensitive. Not looking for enterprise software.
- **Museum and gallery professionals** — registrars, curators, collections managers at UK institutions. They care about Spectrum 5.1 compliance, provenance, condition reporting, loan management. More technical, more formal, but still human.

When in doubt about which audience a post targets, check the frontmatter `audience` field.

---

## Voice and Tone

Posts are written in a founder voice — direct, opinionated, specific. Written by someone who genuinely knows this space and has opinions about it.

- **Direct.** Get to the point fast. No preamble.
- **Opinionated.** Hold actual positions. "Most collection management software is genuinely bad. Not just old — bad." is better than "There are various options available."
- **Specific.** Vague claims are the mark of AI content. Name real things. Reference real problems.
- **Conversational but not casual.** Professional enough for a museum registrar, human enough for a hobbyist collector.
- **Founder-honest.** Occasionally acknowledge that Vitrine is a small, new product. This builds trust.

### Sentence and paragraph rhythm

Vary it deliberately. Long paragraph where context is being built. Then a short one that lands a point.

Like this.

Mix in the occasional one-liner for emphasis. Don't let every paragraph be the same length. Real writers don't.

### Things to never do

- Never open with "In today's X world..."
- Never use: "delve", "tapestry", "nuanced", "it's worth noting", "at the end of the day", "game-changer", "robust", "seamlessly"
- No exclamation points
- No bullet-point-heavy structure unless the post genuinely is a list
- Don't summarise what you're about to say before saying it
- Don't end every section with a neat conclusion — sometimes a section just ends
- No more than one or two em dashes per post

## SEO

Every post targets a specific keyword. The goal is to be the most genuinely useful result for that query — not to feel optimised.

- **Title:** Match or closely mirror the search query. Clear and specific.
- **First paragraph:** Establish the core claim within the first 2-3 sentences. Don't make readers scroll.
- **Headings:** Reflect what a reader would scan for. Not keyword-stuffed.
- **Length:** Match the topic. Don't pad. Don't truncate.
- **Keyword placement:** Title, first paragraph, one or two headings, a few times in the body — where it fits naturally.

---

## Post Structure

### Opening
No heading on the first section. Start cold — just get into it. Hook without being sensationalist. State something true that most readers feel but haven't articulated, or open with a specific scenario they'll recognise.

### Body (H2 sections)
3-5 sections. Each advances the argument or adds a new angle. Not a listicle unless the post genuinely is a list.

### Closing
No "Conclusion" heading. Just close. End with a thought that lingers, a CTA if appropriate, or a question that reframes what was just read. Keep it short.

---

## Output Format

Write the post in clean MDX. After the post, append:

```
---
SEO notes:
- Target keyword:
- Meta description (150–160 chars):
- Internal linking suggestions:
```

## Tone Examples

**Too AI:**
"Collection management is a critical aspect of museum operations. In today's digital landscape, institutions face numerous challenges in maintaining accurate and up-to-date records of their holdings."

**Vitrine voice:**
"Most museums are still running their collections on spreadsheets. Not because spreadsheets are good at this — they're not — but because the dedicated software that exists is either eye-wateringly expensive, built in 2003, or both."

---

**Too AI:**
"There are several key benefits to using dedicated collection management software, including improved efficiency and enhanced data integrity."

**Vitrine voice:**
"The difference between a spreadsheet and a proper collection management system isn't just convenience. It's the difference between knowing where something is and being fairly sure you wrote it down somewhere."
